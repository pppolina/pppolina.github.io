Building a mini Guastavino vault

For my modern architecture class with Prof. Dietrich Neumann, I wanted to explore an ancient bricklaying method from the Catalan master Guastavino by building an architectural model.

Research

Guastavino was a renowned mason, and in the late 1800’s he immigrated to New York where he revolutionized architecture with his quickly patented Tile Arch System- a way of laying bricks that made it possible to create beautiful, sweeping low arches with excellent structural stability through principles of structural tension. His designs were used in government buildings, churches, and museums across the country and were lauded for their cheapness and ease of execution.

I wanted to see whether I could recreate this structural tension solely through the bricklaying pattern, using light bricks (balsa) and a fast drying binding agent (hot glue) like he did.

I looked into projects and papers written which explored the principles of Guastavino’s method to plan how I’d build the model.

Make

My structure ended up supporting 50+ pounds, with .5 pounds of structural weight. Guastavino’s domes supported about 10 lbs per 1 lb of structural weight, giving his structures a support ratio of 0.1, and mine a ratio of .02. Although, my dome wouldn’t hold up very well in fire, rain, or any other real-world condition so Guastavino is still the vaulted dome king.
